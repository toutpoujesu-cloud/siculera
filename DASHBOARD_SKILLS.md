# Dashboard Partner Skills

## 1. Product and Business Mastery
- Must master KPI thinking. They need to know the difference between vanity metrics, operational metrics, lagging indicators, leading indicators, diagnostic metrics, and executive summary metrics.
- Must master business translation. When leadership says “we need visibility,” they should be able to identify whether that means alerts, forecasting, drill-downs, exception tracking, benchmarking, or workflow intervention.
- Must master dashboard purpose types. They need to know how executive dashboards differ from operational dashboards, analytical dashboards, monitoring dashboards, compliance dashboards, and admin consoles.
- Must master decision-support design. A dashboard is not decoration; it must help someone decide, act, approve, escalate, compare, investigate, or monitor.
- Must master ambiguity reduction. They should know how to turn vague goals into measurable product requirements, user flows, module priorities, and rollout stages.
- Must master cross-functional business contexts. They should be comfortable with dashboards in finance, operations, logistics, support, sales, CRM, supply chain, risk, compliance, HR, ecommerce, and executive reporting.
- Must master prioritization under pressure. They should know what belongs on day one, what belongs behind drill-downs, and what should not be built at all.
- Must master stakeholder alignment. They should be able to reconcile what executives want to see, what operators need to do, what compliance requires, and what engineering can realistically ship.

## 2. Information Architecture Mastery
- Must master hierarchy. They need to know how to structure dense information so the most important signals appear first, secondary detail supports them, and deep detail remains accessible without cluttering the screen.
- Must master navigation systems. They should be able to design left nav, top nav, tab systems, sectional navigation, sub-navigation, and cross-module movement for large platforms.
- Must master drill-down logic. They need to know when a user should see overview, detail, modal, drawer, subpage, or inline expansion.
- Must master density management. They should know how to handle extremely large data surfaces without making the interface exhausting.
- Must master mental models. They need to organize screens in ways that match how users think, not just how databases are structured.
- Must master multi-role architecture. Superadmins, managers, analysts, support staff, finance users, and regional users often need different dashboard visibility and different levels of control.
- Must master empty, partial, and error states. Enterprise systems often have incomplete data, delayed integrations, missing permissions, or outdated records, and the IA must still remain coherent.
- Must master discoverability. Important actions, filters, exports, and diagnostic tools must be easy to find without overloading the interface.

## 3. UX Mastery for Complex Systems
- Must master workflows, not just pages. They should think in end-to-end flows: login, review, filter, inspect, update, confirm, log, notify, and report.
- Must master table UX. This includes sortable columns, saved views, bulk actions, sticky headers, selection patterns, inline actions, pagination, server-side filtering, and large dataset usability.
- Must master filters. They need to design filters that are powerful but understandable, with clear states, quick resets, dependency logic, and shareable views where needed.
- Must master search. They should understand global search, scoped search, faceted search, fuzzy search, exact match behavior, and “no results” recovery.
- Must master forms in admin contexts. This includes complex validation, inline errors, dependencies, edit locks, drafts, autosave, destructive actions, and audit visibility.
- Must master role-based workflow safety. Dangerous actions need confirmation patterns, undo strategies, access control clarity, and prevention of accidental damage.
- Must master notification design. Alerts, warnings, confirmations, failures, system messages, and passive status indicators must be distinct and meaningful.
- Must master progressive disclosure. High-complexity interfaces must reveal more only when needed, without hiding critical information.
- Must master onboarding and learnability. Even enterprise tools need smooth first-use experiences, guided empty states, and intuitive terminology.
- Must master task efficiency. Frequent users should be able to complete repetitive workflows quickly, with minimal clicks, friction, or cognitive overhead.
- Must master user trust. Data-heavy systems live or die on credibility. Labels, timestamps, units, sources, and status indicators must be unambiguous.
- Must master usability under stress. Some dashboards are used during incidents, escalations, fulfillment breakdowns, or executive scrutiny. The experience must remain stable and legible in those moments.

## 4. UI and Visual Design Mastery
- Must master visual hierarchy at a professional level. They need to control attention with typography, spacing, contrast, grouping, density, and layout rhythm.
- Must master dashboard composition. A dashboard is not a random grid of cards; it needs balance, pacing, relationship between modules, and narrative flow.
- Must master enterprise visual credibility. Fortune 500 interfaces need to feel dependable, polished, mature, and premium.
- Must master typography. They should know how to use scale, weight, spacing, casing, numerical alignment, and tabular figures for data-heavy surfaces.
- Must master color systems. Not just branding colors, but semantic color, state color, contrast rules, accessibility-safe palettes, and restrained use of emphasis.
- Must master component refinement. Buttons, tables, tags, pills, filters, cards, sidebars, tabs, banners, and modals should feel part of a coherent system.
- Must master responsive dashboard design. Desktop-first does not mean desktop-only. They must know how complex systems collapse on tablet and mobile without becoming unusable.
- Must master iconography. Icons must support meaning, not replace labels when clarity would suffer.
- Must master motion restraint. Animations should improve comprehension and focus, not slow the user down or make the interface feel ornamental.
- Must master whitespace. Enterprise interfaces need density, but not crowding. They should know how to make rich interfaces breathe.
- Must master visual consistency without monotony. The UI should feel systematic, but still create emphasis where business-critical content deserves attention.

## 5. Data Visualization Mastery
- Must master chart selection. They need to know exactly when to use bar, line, area, stacked bar, scatter, heatmap, donut, funnel, bullet chart, timeline, map, sparkline, KPI card, or no chart at all.
- Must master analytical honesty. Visualizations must not distort comparisons, exaggerate changes, or hide uncertainty.
- Must master comparison patterns. Period-over-period, cohort, target-vs-actual, region-vs-region, exception-vs-baseline, and segment-vs-total comparisons should be handled clearly.
- Must master anomaly visibility. A good dashboard expert knows how to make outliers and operational risk visible without overwhelming the default view.
- Must master labeling and annotation. Chart titles, axis labels, legends, units, tooltips, benchmarks, and explanatory notes must remove ambiguity.
- Must master dense data readability. Many dashboards need to show large volumes of data while preserving scanning speed and comprehension.
- Must master drill-through analytics. Users often need to move from aggregated insight to root-cause detail in one or two steps.
- Must master time-series interpretation. They need to understand smoothing, granularity, seasonality, grouping, and date range behavior.
- Must master geographic and regional views when relevant. Maps must only be used when geography meaningfully helps the decision.
- Must master mixed-surface design. Dashboards often combine metrics, tables, charts, statuses, alerts, and actions, and those pieces must work together.

## 6. Frontend Engineering Mastery
- Must master HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and TypeScript at senior level.
- Must master responsive layout systems using grid, flexbox, container behavior, and breakpoint strategy for complex admin surfaces.
- Must master component-driven frontend architecture. They should build reusable, predictable, testable UI structures rather than page-specific hacks.
- Must master state handling. They need to understand local state, server state, async loading, optimistic updates, stale data, and UI synchronization.
- Must master API integration. They should handle loading, retries, pagination, auth tokens, partial data, backend inconsistencies, and errors gracefully.
- Must master performance. This includes render performance, bundle discipline, virtualization for large tables, efficient filtering, asset optimization, and perceived speed.
- Must master accessibility implementation. Semantic markup, keyboard navigation, focus management, ARIA only when needed, screen-reader support, and color-contrast compliance must be second nature.
- Must master authentication surfaces. Login flows, token handling, session expiry, refresh logic, logout behavior, and protected-route behavior must be reliable.
- Must master form engineering. Validation, touched/dirty states, conditional fields, dependent inputs, async validation, and failure recovery need to be robust.
- Must master testing discipline. They should know unit, integration, smoke, and UI regression testing patterns for product-critical interfaces.
- Must master code quality. Naming, architecture, organization, and maintainability must support team growth.
- Must master production hardening. Logging, error boundaries, fallback behavior, telemetry hooks, and defensive coding matter in enterprise dashboards.

## 7. Backend and Systems Awareness
- They do not need to be a backend specialist, but must understand backend realities deeply enough to design and build responsibly.
- Must understand relational data structures and how they affect UI behavior.
- Must understand API contracts, pagination strategies, filtering on the server, sorting logic, permissions, and audit trails.
- Must understand authentication and authorization. Not just login, but RBAC, scoped permissions, action restrictions, and visibility rules.
- Must understand file handling, exports, imports, background jobs, notifications, and long-running processes if the product depends on them.
- Must understand performance constraints from database size, query latency, network variability, and legacy backend structures.
- Must understand data integrity. A dashboard that allows editing operational data needs safe update patterns, concurrency awareness, and trustworthy feedback.
- Must understand logs, monitoring, and observability enough to diagnose integration or production issues with engineers.

## 8. Design System Mastery
- Must master tokens. Color, spacing, radius, type scale, elevation, motion, and semantic aliases must be systematized.
- Must master component standards. They should know what makes a component reusable, governable, and adaptable without becoming bloated.
- Must master system documentation. Design choices must be understandable by designers, developers, QA, and product teams.
- Must master component variation control. They need to prevent the system from turning into twenty slightly different buttons, tables, and filters.
- Must master handoff quality. Specs, behavior, edge cases, states, responsive behavior, accessibility expectations, and usage guidance should be explicit.
- Must master system governance. They should know how to evolve a design system under real team pressure without breaking consistency.

## 9. Content Design and UX Writing Mastery
- Must master naming. Labels, navigation titles, column names, filter names, button text, and setting descriptions must be clear and business-accurate.
- Must master microcopy. Empty states, helper text, placeholders, tooltips, status text, warnings, confirmations, and success messages must reduce confusion.
- Must master error language. Messages should explain what happened, why, and what the user can do next.
- Must master tone control. Enterprise products need clarity, professionalism, and confidence without sounding robotic.
- Must master content hierarchy. Long settings pages, admin forms, and dense operational screens need scannable structure.
- Must master localization awareness. Even if they do not translate directly, they should design copy and layout that can survive expansion and multi-language support.
- Must master domain language. They need to use terms the business and users actually understand, not generic product language.

## 10. Accessibility Mastery
- Must master WCAG fundamentals and real implementation, not checkbox familiarity.
- Must master keyboard-only usage for complex tables, forms, filters, and modals.
- Must master screen-reader behavior in dynamic interfaces.
- Must master accessible chart alternatives or supporting summaries.
- Must master focus order, visible focus, focus trapping in overlays, and focus return after actions.
- Must master semantic structure for headings, regions, navigation, forms, tables, and alerts.
- Must master contrast, text sizing, and non-color-based state communication.
- Must master accessible error handling and validation messaging.

## 11. Research and Validation Mastery
- Must master stakeholder interviewing. They should know how to extract real needs instead of copying requested screens literally.
- Must master workflow mapping. They should be able to diagram how work actually happens across people, systems, and decisions.
- Must master usability testing for complex enterprise tasks.
- Must master heuristic evaluation. They should quickly spot structural, interaction, and comprehension failures.
- Must master synthesis. Research findings should become priorities, interface changes, and product decisions.
- Must master iterative refinement. They should know how to improve the product over time based on usage and feedback, not just launch it.

## 12. Enterprise Delivery Mastery
- Must master working in legacy environments. Fortune 500 systems are rarely greenfield and rarely clean.
- Must master working under constraints. Security, compliance, procurement, branding, and internal politics often shape what can be shipped.
- Must master multi-stakeholder communication. They need to present to executives, work with PMs, align with engineering, and handle feedback from operators.
- Must master phased rollout strategy. They should know how to ship core value first and expand without destabilizing the platform.
- Must master documentation and decision traceability.
- Must master high-stakes revisions. When legal, compliance, or executive priorities shift, they should adapt without destroying product quality.
- Must master platform thinking. The best person can think beyond a page and shape a product suite.

## 13. Experience I Would Require
- Deep experience shipping dashboards that people actually use to run operations, not just visual concepts.
- Real ownership of both design decisions and implementation tradeoffs.
- Experience with admin systems, analytics systems, or internal tools where accuracy and speed matter.
- Experience improving broken or legacy systems, not only greenfield launches.
- Experience with role-based platforms and protected workflows.
- Experience balancing visual refinement with operational clarity.
- Experience handling data-driven products with tables, search, filters, charts, exports, and settings-heavy interfaces.
- Experience working with engineering constraints and backend realities.
- Experience presenting work to decision-makers who care about business outcomes, not just aesthetics.

## 14. What “Mastery” Looks Like in Practice
- They can look at a broken dashboard and diagnose structural, UX, visual, and technical problems quickly.
- They know which metrics belong on the main view and which should be hidden behind drill-downs.
- They can turn a chaotic admin panel into a system that feels calm, clear, and efficient.
- They can explain every design choice in terms of user decisions, business value, and implementation tradeoffs.
- They can build what they design.
- They can improve a product without making it generic.
- They can work fast without becoming sloppy.
- They do not confuse complexity with sophistication.
- They do not confuse visual polish with usability.
- They do not need constant direction to make senior-level decisions.

## 15. The Ideal Candidate
The ideal person is a hybrid of:
- senior product designer
- senior UX architect
- senior frontend engineer
- data visualization specialist
- enterprise content strategist
- design-system thinker
- business-savvy product operator
